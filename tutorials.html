My plan is to use this page to share educational resources that I have found useful, and to share some insights of my own. Hopefully this will help others out there trying to learn theory through self-study.

The core mathematics that any working scientist should be familiar with is included in a set of three lecture courses in the typical undergraduate curriculum: Linear Algebra, Calculus, and Differential Equations. I learned these subjects using MIT OpenCourseWare, and then refined my knowledge by working on projects and setting fun problems for myself. Here is a breakdown:

Linear Algebra, taught by Gilbert Strang. When it comes to maths, I was a late starter. I followed this course at 23 after not doing any maths since finishing high school at 16, and it opened my eyes to how beautiful the subject can be. Gilbert Strang's enthusiasm for the subject shines, and because of the clarity with which he expresses the key ideas, that enthusiasm is infectious. If you're like me, and are trying to learn advanced maths after hating it in school, or after a long break, there is no better (re)introduction than this series of video lectures. Strang's textbooks are also excellent and I highly recommend them for self-study.

Single Variable Calculus, taught by David Jensen. I have heard that nobody enjoys learning calculus, and nobody enjoys teaching calculus. That definitely fits with my own experiences. I personally found this lecture series to be a total slog compared to Strang's linear algebra, but in the long run it has been very worthwhile.

Differential Equations, taught by Arthur Mattuck. This course of video lectures just flew by for me; I loved it! Most of the content is practically very useful for analysing real problems, and the instructors highlight a whole bunch of interesting applications. For me, manipulating and studying differential equations was where learning calculus really started to pay off.
