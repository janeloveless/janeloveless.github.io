Research

I am broadly interested in the theoretical physics of animal behaviour. I want to see how far we can go in understanding living, behaving organisms by applying the same tools that are used to understand non-living systems. I suppose this reflects a conviction that there should be no huge difference between the two. My work draws on analytical mechanics, dynamical systems, statistical mechanics; I am being increasingly drawn into quantum field theory, condensed matter theory, and kinetic theory.

During my PhD I built biomechanical models of the Drosophila larva (fruitfly maggot) which suggested the physics of the animal's body should give rise to deterministic yet inherently unpredictable movement. I harnessed this description to build elegant neuromechanical models of the animals' exploration behaviour, both in the presence and absence of external sensory cues.

Since then, I've been engaged in close experimental collaborations with Claudio Alonso and Tomoko Ohyama, developing simple equilibrium effective field theories of larval behaviours. Amazingly, our approach allows us to explain and predict detailed features of animal behaviour based only on symmetry considerations, without having to say much about the nervous system at all.

More recently, I've started applying similar approaches to understand the behaviour of the nematode worm C. elegans, and I'm continuing to try and probe exactly why these "simple" theoretical approaches work in the first place.

Teaching

I've taught a range of topics within biomedical sciences, informatics, and robotics.

I am particularly interested in teaching mathematics and the rudiments of (formal, mathematical) theoretical science to biologists. We are generally not taught how to build or even interpret formal theories during our training, which is a great disservice -- I strongly believe that mathematics is the best language we have to describe nature.

I am always happy to tutor other biologists (at any stage of their career) in mathematics, physics, and coding. I can also provide self-teaching references, and guide you through the process of teaching yourself about these areas. Shoot me an email at jane-loveless [at] oist.jp if you are interested. If we cover topics which I believe should be of general interest I will add my notes to this website here.
